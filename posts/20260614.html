i recently launched fretboardcodex.com, a theory-driven fretboard reference that players can use when they're practicing or writing. pick a tonic and a scale and the whole page rewires itself: notes, diatonic chords, borrowed chords from parallel modes, voicings, circle of fifths/fourths, and a practice routine tailored to the key. one global key center, everything else follows.

fundamentals of the design

a few choices that shaped the whole thing:

one global key center. the top of the page picks a tonic and a scale. every panel below reads from that. change the key in one spot and the whole page updates.

theory you can trust. all the note math runs through tonal.js rather than hand-rolled lookups, so the spelling is correct in every key (f♯ major shows e♯, not f) and the roman numerals follow whatever family you're in — major, minor, or one of the modes. seventh-chord toggle, parallel/relative-key swap with one click.

intervals as the default language. notes are useful but intervals are what your hands actually learn. roots are filled squares, 3rds and 5ths and 7ths are filled circles, other scale tones are open circles. shape and label always carry the meaning. there's also a monochrome toggle for printing or for colorblind-safe viewing.

CAGED as a window, not a wall chart. instead of five static diagrams, the CAGED row reframes the live fretboard around the chosen shape. the I-chord tones are colored, the rest of the scale stays outlined behind them. switch shapes and the window slides up the neck!

voicings, not just shapes. focus any diatonic chord and a voicings panel opens above the fretboard: triads, 7ths, and extensions (9ths, 11ths, 13ths) you can toggle on/off and see overlaid on the neck.

quiet visuals, slightly broken. six color palettes, each tuned for day and night. switch any time from the floating palette button.

12 / 15 / 22 frets, lefty mirror, drag-to-reorder panels. the small ergonomic things mattered more than i expected. the lower three panels (modal interchange, circle of fifths, practice) can be reordered with a click-and-drag.

how to use it

three ways i actually use it day to day:

as a practice companion. pick a key, generate a routine in the practice panel. each prompt has a hint and a jump-to button that scrolls to the relevant panel (the harmony, the fretboard, the interchange tabs).

as a writing tool. when i'm sketching a part and want a chord that isn't quite diatonic, i flip to the interchange panel and see what parallel dorian, phrygian, mixolydian, or harmonic minor would lend me. click the chord, it focuses on the fretboard.

as a key map. the key card shows notes, degrees, intervals, family, parallel and relative. the circle of fifths shows where you are and what's one move away.
